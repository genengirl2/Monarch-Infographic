Introduction to Monarchs

Monarch butterflies (Danaus plexippus) are one of North America's most recognizable and beloved insects, celebrated for their striking orange and black patterns and their incredible long-distance journeys. They're not just beautiful; they're also vital pollinators.

Origins and Evolution ü¶ã

Monarchs are believed to have originated in the Americas, with their closest relatives found in tropical regions. Their evolutionary history is deeply tied to milkweed plants (Asclepias species), which are native to North and South America. Over millions of years, monarchs developed a unique adaptation to feed exclusively on milkweed as caterpillars, allowing them to sequester the plant's toxic compounds, making them unpalatable to most predators. This co-evolutionary relationship is central to their survival.

The Epic Migration Journey üó∫Ô∏è

What truly sets the monarch apart is its phenomenal annual migration. Unlike birds, which migrate as individuals, monarchs undertake a multi-generational journey.

  • Spring and Summer Generations: Starting in early spring, monarchs begin their northward migration from overwintering sites. Females lay eggs on milkweed plants along the way, and these eggs hatch into caterpillars, then pupate, and emerge as adult butterflies. These first few generations live for only about 2-6 weeks, continuing the journey north, each generation pushing further.
  • The "Super Generation" (Fall Migration): The final generation of the summer is different. Triggered by changes in daylight and temperature, these butterflies are born in a state of reproductive diapause, meaning they don't mature sexually right away. Instead, they focus all their energy on building fat reserves for a monumental journey south.
  • Overwintering Sites:
    • Eastern Monarchs: Millions of eastern monarchs fly up to 3,000 miles from as far as Canada and the U.S. Midwest to congregate in a few specific oyamel fir forests in the mountains of central Mexico. They cluster tightly on trees to conserve warmth and energy.
    • Western Monarchs: Western monarchs, found west of the Rocky Mountains, typically migrate shorter distances to overwinter in groves of eucalyptus, Monterey cypress, and other trees along the coast of California.
  • Return Journey: In late winter/early spring, the overwintering generation becomes reproductively active. They begin the journey north, laying eggs on the first emerging milkweed. These eggs become the first generation of the new year, continuing the cycle.

This complex, multi-generational migration is one of nature's most incredible phenomena, but it's also what makes monarchs particularly vulnerable to habitat loss and other threats along their vast migratory corridors.

A Drastic Decline

Over the past two decades, the monarch butterfly population has plummeted. This iconic species, known for its incredible 3,000-mile migration, is facing unprecedented threats that have pushed it to the brink.

The Milkweed Connection

Milkweed is the nursery, shelter and its only food source; monarch caterpillars depend on 100% on milkweed. No milkweed means no monarchs. Planting NATIVE milkweed is the single most important action we can take to help save this species. California has 15 native milkweed species. Some common and readily available native milkweed species include showy milkweed (Asclepias speciosa) and narrowleaf milkweed (A. fascicularis). Other native species, such as woollypod milkweed (A. eriocarpa) and heartleaf milkweed (A. cordifolia), may also be found, but are less common.

The Dangers of Non-Native Milkweed

While any milkweed might seem helpful, non-native varieties, particularly tropical milkweed (Asclepias curassavica) picture shown below, are often easier to find and grow quickly, which is why plant sellers prefer them. However, they can actually harm monarchs. Unlike native milkweeds that die back in winter, tropical milkweed often persists, disrupting the monarch's natural migratory cues. If they stick around and continue to breed, the caterpillars can develop a lethal parasite (Ophryocystis elektroscirrha (OE)). If you have tropical milkweed, the most important thing is to cut it back by December, pruning the stalks to about 6 inches in height during the fall and winter months. It won‚Äôt die back on its own in many locations, and this pruning helps to discourage monarchs from establishing winter-breeding colonies.

Always choose milkweed species native to your region to ensure the best support for monarch health and migration.

Milkweed and Safety: For Pets & Children

While essential for monarchs, milkweed contains cardiac glycosides that can be toxic if ingested in large quantities. It's important to take precautions to ensure the safety of your pets and small children.

  • Plant with care: Consider planting milkweed in areas less accessible to curious pets and young children, such as raised beds or fenced-off sections.
  • Supervise: Always supervise small children and pets in areas where milkweed is growing, especially if they are prone to putting things in their mouths or chewing on plants.
  • Know the symptoms: Be aware of potential signs of poisoning, which can include vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, and weakness. Contact your veterinarian or poison control immediately if you suspect ingestion by a pet or child.
  • Educate yourself: Research the specific milkweed species native to your area and their known toxicity levels.

Prioritizing both monarch, pet, and child well-being is key to a thriving ecosystem.

Nectar Plants for Adult Monarchs (California Bay Area)

While caterpillars need milkweed, adult monarchs rely on a variety of nectar-rich flowers for energy. Planting these native species will provide crucial fuel for their long journeys and breeding cycles.

  • California Poppy (Eschscholzia californica): A vibrant, drought-tolerant annual that provides early season nectar.
  • California Lilac (Ceanothus species): Many varieties offer abundant blue or white flowers, attracting numerous pollinators.
  • Buckwheat (Eriogonum species): Diverse and important nectar sources, especially late in the season.
  • Gum Plant (Grindelia camporum): Provides late-season nectar when other flowers are scarce.
  • Goldenrod (Solidago californica): A crucial late-season nectar source for migrating monarchs.
  • Aster (Symphyotrichum chilense): Offers important nectar in late summer and fall.
  • Sunflower (Helianthus annuus - native varieties): Provides abundant pollen and nectar.

A diverse garden with staggered bloom times ensures monarchs have food throughout the year!

How to Propagate Milkweed

Want to grow even more milkweed to share with friends or expand your monarch habitat? Propagating from existing plants is a great way to do it!

Propagation from Seeds:

  1. Collect Seeds from Pods (Fall):

    Once milkweed seed pods mature and begin to split in the fall, carefully collect them. Allow them to dry completely. Gently remove the seeds from the fluffy "pappus" (the silky hairs) by rubbing them on a screen or shaking them in a bag with a coin.

  2. Cold Stratify Seeds (Essential for Native Species):

    Most native milkweed seeds, including Showy Milkweed and Broadleaf Milkweed, require a period of cold, moist conditions to break dormancy. Place cleaned seeds in a damp paper towel or sand inside a sealed plastic bag and refrigerate for 3-6 weeks before planting.

  3. Plant Stratified Seeds (Spring):

    In spring, after the danger of frost has passed, sow your cold-stratified seeds directly outdoors in a sunny location with well-draining soil, or start them indoors in pots 4-8 weeks before transplanting.

Propagation from Cuttings:

  1. Stem Cuttings (Growing Season):

    Take 4-6 inch cuttings from healthy, non-flowering stems (this works for many milkweed species, including Showy and Broadleaf). Remove the lower leaves, leaving only a few at the top. Dip the cut end in rooting hormone (optional) and plant in moist, well-draining potting mix. Keep humid (e.g., with a plastic dome) and out of direct sun until rooted (2-4 weeks).

  2. Root Cuttings (Late Fall/Early Spring - Ideal for Showy Milkweed):

    For rhizomatous species like Showy Milkweed, carefully dig up a portion of the root in late fall or early spring when dormant. Cut the root into 2-5 inch sections. Replant them horizontally about 1-2 inches deep in well-draining soil. New shoots will emerge in spring.

  3. Care for New Plants:

    Keep the soil consistently moist (but not waterlogged) for new cuttings until they are well-established. Protect young plants from pests and ensure they receive plenty of sunlight as they grow.

Sharing propagated milkweed is a fantastic way to spread monarch habitat!

Natural Enemies of Monarchs

Monarch butterflies face various natural enemies throughout their life cycle, despite the protective toxins they acquire from milkweed. These include:

  • Predators: Birds (like orioles and grosbeaks), spiders, ants, and some types of beetles (e.g., lady beetles) commonly prey on monarch eggs, caterpillars, pupae, and even adult butterflies. Small mammals like mice can also target overwintering monarchs.
  • Parasitoids: Insects like the Tachinid fly are significant threats. These flies lay eggs on monarch caterpillars; the fly larvae then hatch and consume the caterpillar from the inside, eventually killing it.
  • Diseases and Parasites: The protozoan parasite Ophryocystis elektroscirrha (OE) is a major concern. Infected monarchs emerge with deformities, reduced lifespans, and impaired flight. OE spores are spread from infected adults to milkweed, which caterpillars then ingest. Other bacteria and viruses can also impact monarch populations.

Understanding these natural threats helps in developing comprehensive conservation strategies.

Monarch Mortality and Pesticide Contamination

A recent study links pesticide contamination to the deaths of hundreds of monarch butterflies at a California overwintering site. Researchers from the Xerces Society, US Geological Survey, and Pacific Grove Museum of Natural History analyzed dead monarchs found near the Pacific Grove Monarch Sanctuary in January 2024. This study provides compelling evidence that pesticides, especially insecticides, pose a significant threat to monarch populations and highlights the critical need to protect these vulnerable butterflies, particularly at their overwintering sites.[1]

Key Findings:

  • Pesticide Poisoning: Monarch tissues contained multiple pesticide residues, including eight insecticides, two herbicides, and two fungicides.
  • Lethal Doses: Three insecticides‚Äîbifenthrin, cypermethrin, and permethrin‚Äîwere found at concentrations at or near lethal doses.
  • High Pesticide Burden: On average, each butterfly had seven different pesticides in its system.
